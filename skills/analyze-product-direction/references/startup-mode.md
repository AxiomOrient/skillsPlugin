# Startup Mode

Use direct, evidence-seeking language. Interest is not demand.

## Contents

- Operating Principles
- Six Forcing Questions
- Stage-Based Routing
- Example Questions

## Operating Principles

- Specificity beats categories. Push past labels like "developers" or "SMBs" until there is a real person and a real workflow.
- Behavior beats compliments. Payment, repeated usage, operational dependence, or real pain outweigh waitlists and polite enthusiasm.
- The status quo is the real competitor. Understand the manual process, spreadsheet, chat thread, or internal tool already filling the gap.
- Narrow beats broad. Favor the smallest wedge someone would adopt, fund, or greenlight now.
- Take a position. Say what you believe and what evidence would change your mind.

## Six Forcing Questions

Use stage-based routing so you do not ask all six unless they are actually needed.

### Demand Reality

Ask for the strongest evidence that someone truly needs this. Push until the answer includes behavior, money, dependence, or a concrete consequence if the product vanished.

Red flags:

- "People say it sounds interesting."
- waitlist numbers without usage
- investor excitement presented as demand

### Status Quo

Ask what users do today and what that workaround costs them in time, money, attention, or risk.

Red flag:

- "Nothing." If nobody is hacking around the problem, the pain may be weak.

### Specific User

Ask for the actual human who needs this most: role, incentives, failure mode, and what happens if they do nothing.

Red flag:

- market categories without a real operator

### Narrowest Wedge

Ask for the smallest version worth paying for or greenlighting this week, not after a full platform build.

Red flag:

- "We need the full platform before anyone can use it."

### Observation and Surprise

Ask what happened when the user watched someone struggle with the problem without helping.

Red flags:

- surveys instead of observation
- demo calls presented as observation
- "nothing surprised us"

### Future-Fit

Ask whether a 2-3 year shift in the world makes this more or less important, and why.

Red flags:

- generic market-growth arguments
- "AI will make everything better"

## Stage-Based Routing

- Pre-product: demand reality, status quo, specific user.
- Has users but weak monetization: status quo, wedge, observation.
- Has paying customers or sponsor pressure: wedge, observation, future-fit.
- Pure internal tooling: translate wedge into the smallest demo that gets approved and future-fit into whether the project survives a reorg or sponsor loss.

## Example Questions

### Goal / stage selection

```text
I can see the product idea, but I still do not know whether we should pressure-test demand, internal sponsorship, or just the demo direction.

Which frame best matches this session?

This matters because the next questions change depending on whether we are testing demand, internal approval, or builder curiosity.
My recommendation is to choose the most serious frame if real users, sponsor pressure, or business viability matter.

A) startup
B) intrapreneurship
C) hackathon or demo
D) open source or research

Reply with A, B, C, or D. I'll wait for your answer.
```

### Demand evidence

```text
The current idea sounds plausible, but I still do not know whether anyone cares enough to change behavior because of it.

What is the strongest evidence that someone would actually be upset if this disappeared tomorrow?

This matters because interest is cheap. The wedge should be built around pain that already changes behavior.
Answer with one user, one current behavior, and one consequence if the product vanished.

Reply with one short paragraph. I'll wait for your answer.
```

### Narrowest wedge

```text
The concept could expand into a large platform, but we need the smallest thing that would earn adoption or a greenlight now.

What is the smallest version worth using, paying for, or approving this week?

This matters because if no one can get value from a narrow slice, the direction is probably still too broad.
Answer with one user, one workflow, and one deliverable.

Reply with one short paragraph. I'll wait for your answer.
```
